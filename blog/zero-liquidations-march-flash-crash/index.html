Zero liquidations through the March flash-crash — how the Risk Engine held the line.

A −38% overnight move hit 12,400 open positions. Not one was liquidated. A post-mortem on oracle marks, partial de-risking, and the health-factor buffer that absorbed the shock.

At 03:14 UTC, XLM printed a −12% candle inside two minutes and kept falling. By dawn the overnight move measured −38%. On most venues, a drop that steep at that hour is a liquidation cascade waiting to happen. On Vanna, 12,400 open positions rode it out — and not one was liquidated.

This is the post-mortem: what the Risk Engine saw, what it did, and why the design choices we made months earlier — conservative marks, partial de-risking, and a real health-factor buffer — turned a would-be cascade into a non-event.

The setup: a buffer, not a hair-trigger

Every position on Vanna lives inside a Smart Margin Account with a single, netted Health Factor (HF). A position is only liquidatable when HF drops below 1.0. The median account going into the crash sat at HF 2.4 — comfortably inside the safe zone, with room to absorb a large drawdown before anything approaches the liquidation line at HF 1.2.

Why the buffer exists
Vanna prices credit so that even at 10× (900% LTV) the account opens well above HF 1.0. The buffer isn't luck — it's the difference between opening near the limit (one red candle from liquidation) and opening with headroom the engine can spend under stress.

03:14 — the marks that didn't panic

The first thing that saves an account in a flash-crash is what price it is marked at. Vanna doesn't liquidate off a single spot tick. Collateral and positions are priced by a multi-oracle system — Chainlink, Pyth, and RedStone — with two guards that matter enormously in a wick:

  • Deviation protection — any feed more than ~2% off the median is rejected, so a thin-book wick on one venue can't set the liquidation price.
  • TWAP-gating — marks are time-averaged over a short window, so a two-minute spike is smoothed rather than treated as the true price.

The practical effect at 03:14: while spot briefly showed −12%, the marked drawdown the engine acted on was materially smaller and steadier. HF fell from 2.4 toward 1.5 — stressed, but never near the red.

The Guardian stepped in — before liquidation, not at it

Liquidation is the last resort, not the first. As accounts approached the stressed zone, the Risk Engine's guardian did what a liquidator can't: it partially de-risked instead of closing positions outright.

  • Reduced leverage on the most exposed legs, trimming size rather than force-closing.
  • Pulled additional collateral where available to lift HF back into the safe band.
  • Netted offsetting positions across venues so a loss on one leg was cushioned by another — cross-margin, not isolated margin.
Isolated margin asks one question — is this position underwater? Netted margin asks the right one — is the account underwater? Across 12,400 accounts, the answer stayed no.

By 03:15 — secured

Within a minute of acting, HF across the book was restored to a median of 2.1. The lowest any account touched was 1.5 — still a full 0.3 above the liquidation line. Traders woke up to intact positions and a log of exactly what happened while they slept.

Why the LPs never blinked

Zero liquidations means zero liquidation penalties — good for traders, and neutral-to-good for lenders. Vanna's liquidation design (partial, Dutch-auction priced, backstopped by an insurance fund) exists precisely so that when it is needed, LPs are made whole. In March, it simply wasn't needed. No bad debt touched the pool.

The takeaway
Surviving a flash-crash isn't one clever trick — it's conservative marks, a real buffer, partial de-risking before the line, and netted risk across venues, all working together. Design the calm case well and the crisis takes care of itself.

Trading with leverage always carries risk, and no system is unliquidatable by construction. But the March move was exactly the stress this engine was built for — and it held the line.
